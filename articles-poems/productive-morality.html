Productivity as a moral concept

Productivity being a moral concept or not, what do I think?

Firstly, I am so confused by how one can even define morality. Not killing a human, not hurting someone, not cheating people, stuff like that, is morality. Not wasting food, or looking down on people, or other things, not performing your family duties, not doing your work well? So, is morality consisted of intentional actions- that one has the capability to perform; actions that harms other beings? Being humble and not bragging. But these are specific examples. Each person has their own morality too. For me, I think saying thank you and please is very important, but isn't that courtesy? What's the difference between courtesy and morality? Courtesy is something that's just nice to have, and it is okay to not have it, doesn't make you a 'bad' person.

Okay, morality is, the concept of good and bad. White and black. Somethings are on a societal level, actions that are universally recognized as immoral. Even if someone has the opposing beliefs, and consider it as moral, it is universally considered as immoral by other people. So, I think, morality is something that is universally recognized. Universal morality has a preference over individual morality, always.

Universal guide book of good and bad things, is what morality is. Why is there a guidebook in the first place? Good question. I think, perhaps, this guidebook emerged, possibly, to keep society together, intact, and functional. Look at the things that are considered immoral - most of them are hurt to other beings, directly or indirectly. Hurt to the cogs in the organization that is society. A set of individuals, each with a unique skill, contributing to the growth of everyone as a whole. You are setting back the society by your immoral actions. And why does that concern you, as an individual? Why does setting the society back affect you? Because that risks you being abandoned from the society perhaps. Risk of you being thrown out, left to defend for yourself, left to learn the skills of every individual in the society to fend yourself off. You can never have the lavish that you had in a cooperating group. You can never excel at everything all alone.

Risk of being abandoned, risk to survival, by doing immoral actions that set the society back, but perhaps not you as an individual. Immoral actions only affect the society, not you as an individual directly. The human response to an immoral action, to an action that is out of one's conscience, is so overwhelming because of year's of conditioning.

Hence, this is morality. A guidebook, for an individual, to live in a society.

So, as long as you bypass that risk to you as an individual, a societal abandonment risk, doing immoral things doesn't hurt you at all. In fact they are reduced to just an event, one of the countless events of your life.

Another good observation is, that, morality is confusing. Some acts can be justified to be moral, like lying to save someone's life. The guidebook only states what actions are right and wrong. Like a dictionary, gives only the meaning of a word, but doesn't really dictate its usage (emotions, nuances and contexts) all of which are essential when knowledge from the dictionary is put to use. However, I shall not consider this important for our discussion further. For us, morality is absolute.

Productivity - considered a gold standard way of living, spending your time, by doing things that are worthwhile. The worthwhile things are decided by external factors, perhaps your environment, or (not always perhaps, but the average way I think), the society. Actions that help you reach success. now what is success?

Phrases like 'wasting time', 'passing time' implies that the time we have has a better use for certain things only. Chores, for example, is considered as something that 'takes up your time'. With the chores example, given how the role of a housemaker is seen as lower than that of a bread-winner.

All in all, I think that productivity has become somewhat of a moral concept in today's capitalist age.
